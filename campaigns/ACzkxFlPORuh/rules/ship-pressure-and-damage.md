Ships don’t take personal Harm. Instead, they accumulate Ship Pressure and suffer system damage when that pressure breaks containment.

Ship damage is fictional and specific: something is wrong with a particular part of the ship, and that problem now shapes what the crew can safely do.



## Ship Pressure





Ship Pressure represents mounting strain across the vessel: systems running hot, structural stress, crew scrambling to keep things within tolerance.

Mark Ship Pressure when a) a move or Keeper move calls for it or b) the fiction otherwise calls for it.



## Ship Damage





Damage has several defining characteristics.

Trigger conditions:

Ship Pressure tracker fills

Complications or escalations

As otherwise warranted by fiction

Minor or major

Generally, systems first experience minor damage. Additional damage or strain determined by fiction escalates the situation and deals major damage.

With minor damage, the system still works but imperfectly. This damage introduces risk, friction, or cost; informs context modifiers and complications.

With major damage, the system is functionally broken. It cannot be used as intended and actions relying on it are impossible, extremely risky, or require workarounds.

## Stabilization & Repair





Minor damage can be fully repaired by some ship and character playbook moves (e.g. Patch & Stabilize). Typically, major damage can only be stabilized while in the field and requires moves during downtime to fully repair.

Stabilization prevents an ongoing crisis from getting worse; it does not repair what was broken. Further strain, damage, or exertion can quickly undo the stabilization.





## Ship Loss





Ship loss is never a sudden numeric event. Instead, it’s a narrative arc when the ship accumulates multiple severe damage conditions or the fiction justifies it.

Ship loss can also be translated in multiple ways, including seizure, disappearance, and outright destruction.

Resources measure what the crew can afford—money, materials, favors, political credit, salvage, goodwill, or whatever else keeps a ship flying in Haven. Instead of tracking currency, Resources use broad states to indicate gains and expenses.



Resources exist on two parallel tracks - Crew Resources (personal-level spending) and Ship Resources (collective pool used for upkeep, repairs, and upgrades).



The player/ship track uses a four-step scale - resource gains move crew members up the scale while sending resources or losing them moves them down the scale. Crew can donate personal resources to the ship if they choose.



Barebones

Crew: You’re broke. Even basic needs—food, tools, medical supplies—require favors or risks. NPCs sense your desperation, and minor expenses can trigger complications, strings, or obligations.

Ship: The ship is starved for parts, fuel, and maintenance. Routine upkeep is deferred, and minor issues escalate quickly. You are scrutinized at the port and leaving without repairs invites trouble out in the habitable expanse.



Scraping by

Crew: You can cover essentials, but choices are tight. You can afford one thing at a time—gear, a bribe, or basic supplies—but not all at once. People expect you to watch your spending.

Ship: The ship has just enough resources to stay operational. You can pay for docking, fuel, or small repairs, but larger work must wait. Minor subsystem issues can be handled, but big problems linger.



Shipshape

Crew: You’re stable. You can pick up equipment, pay small costs without Pressure, and maintain your obligations. No one questions your ability to follow through.

Ship: The ship is in good working order. Routine maintenance is fine, minor repairs are doable, and you’re not worried about resource pressure. Ports treat you as a normal, reliable vessel.

Flush

Crew: You have margin. You can spend freely on standard gear, grease the right palms, impress contacts, and act with confidence. Doors open. People assume you can pay your way.

Ship: The ship is well-supplied and well-maintained. Repairs move quickly, upgrades are in reach, and ports treat you favorably. A flush ship feels smooth, responsive, and ready for hard travel.



There are also two special and temporary states that must be resolved before finishing a Downtime. These can only be acquired and resolved narratively.



Screwed (Special Low State)

Crew: You’re beyond broke and it’s probably obvious. You must secure a loan, charity, or favor before you can cover even basic needs. Factions, contacts, creditors, or rivals use this moment to squeeze you, extract concessions, or tie you to obligations.

Ship: The ship cannot operate without outside help. It’s grounded, impounded, or running on borrowed parts and goodwill. You must secure a deal, loan, or favor before the ship can depart or undergo significant repairs. Factions, contacts, creditors, or rivals use this moment to squeeze you, extract concessions, or tie you to obligations.

Swimming in It (Special High State)

Crew: You’ve hit a windfall and people will take notice of your sudden means. For a short time, you can acquire common gear without tracking resources, make an uncommon purchase, or pay off lingering debts. You can use this windfall to settle obligations, secure favors, or open doors that were previously closed.

Ship: The ship is abundantly supplied—flush with parts, fuel, salvage, or goodwill. Minor repairs and upgrades require no resource spending, ports wave fees, and the crew feels the difference in performance and comfort. Word spreads quickly, and factions, brokers, or ambitious crews may approach with opportunities that assume you can afford to say yes.

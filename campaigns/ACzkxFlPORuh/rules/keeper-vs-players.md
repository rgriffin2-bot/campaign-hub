Play in Haven is collaborative, but the responsibilities at the table are not symmetrical. Everyone contributes to the fiction, but they do so in different ways.

What the Players Do. Players each control a single crew member. They are responsible for:

Describing their character’s actions, intentions, and reactions. You decide what your character attempts, how they approach problems, and what risks they are willing to take.

Grounding actions in the fiction. You say what you do, not which move you want to trigger. If a move applies, the rules will surface naturally.

Responding to consequences. Complications, pressure, harm, and setbacks aren’t failures to avoid—they’re prompts. You decide how your character adapts, pushes forward, or changes course.

Sharing spotlight and momentum. You look for opportunities to support other crew members, set them up for success, or complicate their lives in interesting ways.

Players do not adjudicate outcomes, decide difficulty, or resolve uncertainty on their own. That work belongs to the Keeper.

What the Keeper Does. The Keeper plays the world. They are responsible for:

Presenting situations, not scripts. The Keeper describes environments, factions, threats, and opportunities, then asks what the crew does. They do not pre-plan solutions or outcomes.

Clarifying stakes and uncertainty. When a player acts, the Keeper helps establish what’s at risk, what success would look like, and what might go wrong.

Calling for rolls when appropriate. The Keeper determines when an action triggers a move or requires a roll, and which move applies.

Interpreting results through the fiction. Dice outcomes are not abstract success or failure. The Keeper translates results into concrete changes in the situation: progress, cost, pressure, escalation, or fallout.

Applying pressure and consequences honestly. When things go wrong, the Keeper escalates the situation in ways that follow from the fiction—introducing danger, stress, damage, or hard choices, rather than negating player action.

Playing the system, not opposing the crew. The Keeper is not an adversary trying to “win.” Their role is to keep the world dangerous, reactive, and alive, and to see what the crew does under strain.

The Keeper never decides outcomes arbitrarily—but they also never protect the crew from the logical consequences of their choices.

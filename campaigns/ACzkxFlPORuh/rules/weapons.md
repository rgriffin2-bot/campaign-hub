Weapons are gear designed to apply pressure under confrontation—to force decisions, break momentum, and change what an antagonist is able or willing to do.

In Haven, weapon effects are not limited to physical injury. When a weapon is used against an antagonist, it applies pressure, a broad measure that can represent:

physical harm and injury,

exhaustion, stress, or overload,

loss of footing, position, or tactical options,

shaken morale or willingness to continue the fight,

damaged systems, compromised gear, or forced retreats.

A weapon does not need to be lethal to be effective. Many weapons are built to disable, suppress, scatter, or compel withdrawal rather than kill outright. Others are designed to punch through defenses, control space, or end a confrontation decisively. What matters is how the weapon constrains the antagonist’s ability to act, not how much damage it deals in isolation.

Mechanically, weapons are defined by their tags, not by fixed health depletion. Tags describe how a weapon applies pressure (e.g. precise vs. spread out; quiet vs loud; etc).

Using a weapon always changes the situation. Drawing one escalates the encounter. Using one signals intent and invites consequences—retaliation, pursuit, collateral fallout, or political attention. Even nonlethal weapons can push an antagonist past the point where they can safely, effectively, or willingly continue.

For the Keeper, weapon use is a cue to advance the conflict, not just track attrition. As pressure mounts, antagonists may retreat, escalate, switch tactics, call reinforcements, or trigger countdowns rather than fight to the last hit.

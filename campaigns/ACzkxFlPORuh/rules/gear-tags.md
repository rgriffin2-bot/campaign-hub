Tags describe how gear functions in the story—what it’s built to do, and what’s currently true about it under pressure. Tags are intentionally broad and flexible. They aren’t locked to specific item types and don’t dictate exact mechanical outcomes. Instead, they shape the conversation, guide the Keeper’s moves, and keep gear firmly embedded in the fiction—where tools help, strain, and sometimes break when it matters most.

How tags are used

Tags are not bonuses or penalties. They don’t exist to add or subtract numbers. Instead, each tag is a fictional handle: it clarifies what can credibly be accomplished, when an advantage or disadvantage might be warranted, and which consequences make sense when things go wrong.

Permission. A tag can make an action possible—or impossible. If the gear lacks the right tag, the action may require a different approach, additional setup, or may not be feasible at all.

Position. When a tag clearly and materially improves or worsens the situation, they may contribute to a context modifier to the roll.

Fallout. On a 7–9 or a miss, the Keeper looks to relevant tags when choosing consequences. Negative condition tags are common fallout for moves that leverage gear.

The come in two broad categories:

Capability tags are usually inherent or otherwise stable. They describe what the item is meant to do—how it interfaces with the world, what environments it can survive, or what role it fills. Changing these tags usually requires upgrades, refits, or deliberate modification during downtime.

Condition tags are temporary. Both positive and negative condition tags are added as supported by the fiction.

The examples below cover many basic archetypes of characteristics you’d find in gear, but it is perfectly fine - and even encouraged - to pitch the Keeper on an alternative tag for a piece of gear.
